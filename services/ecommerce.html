The Real Cost of a Bad E-Commerce Website

UK e-commerce is worth over £200 billion annually. Every day your store looks unprofessional, loads slowly, or has a confusing checkout is a day you lose sales to competitors. The average e-commerce conversion rate is 1–3%. A well-designed store with an optimised user journey can reach 4–6% — meaning 2–3x more revenue from the exact same traffic.

We have built e-commerce stores for clothing brands, property platforms, resale marketplaces, food businesses, and niche product sellers across the UK. Our approach is direct: understand your customers, design for conversion, optimise for speed, and build for search.

What Makes a High-Converting E-Commerce Website

Trust Signals

SSL certificate, review integration (Trustpilot, Loox, Google Reviews), clear returns policy, and secure payment badges. Customers need to feel safe before they buy.

Product Page Optimisation

High-quality image galleries, clear variant selectors, urgency elements (limited stock, dispatch time), and persuasive descriptions. A weak product page is the number one source of lost sales.

Frictionless Checkout

Every extra step in checkout costs you sales. We streamline checkout to the minimum number of fields, offer guest checkout, and clearly display shipping costs upfront.

Mobile Shopping Experience

Over 70% of UK e-commerce traffic is on mobile. We design the mobile experience first — thumbs-friendly navigation, large tap targets, and fast image loading.

Site Speed

Amazon found that every 100ms of load time cost them 1% in sales. We compress images, remove unnecessary scripts, and optimise every byte to achieve sub-2-second load times.

Search Engine Visibility

Product pages optimised for Google with keyword-rich titles, meta descriptions, structured product data, and a clean URL structure so your products appear in search results.

E-Commerce Platforms We Build On

Shopify

Our primary recommendation for most product-based UK businesses. Shopify handles payments, inventory, shipping, and updates — so you focus on selling. Custom-designed themes mean your store looks nothing like the defaults. Learn more about our Shopify service.

WooCommerce (WordPress)

Ideal for businesses with complex content needs alongside e-commerce — for example, a store that also maintains a recipe blog, tutorials, or a course platform. Full control over design and functionality. Lower monthly costs than Shopify but requires more technical management.

Custom HTML/PHP

For businesses with highly specialised requirements that no off-the-shelf platform can meet — custom inventory logic, specific API integrations, or unique checkout flows. We build bespoke solutions when platforms cannot deliver.

E-Commerce FAQs

How much does an e-commerce website cost for a small business in the UK?

Our e-commerce stores start from £799 for a custom-designed Shopify store with up to 25 products, payment setup, and launch support. WooCommerce stores start from £1,200. We provide a full itemised quote before any work begins — no hidden costs.

What payment methods can you integrate?

We integrate Stripe, PayPal, Apple Pay, Google Pay, Klarna (buy now pay later), Clearpay, and any major UK payment provider. We set everything up and test it before launch.

Can you help an e-commerce startup with no existing website?

Yes — many of our clients come to us with just a product idea and no website. We help with platform selection, domain setup, branding, store design, product upload, and payment configuration. You walk away with a fully operational store ready to take orders.

Do you handle product photography or copywriting?

We do not provide product photography, but we can advise on image requirements. We offer website copywriting as an add-on service from £150 for key pages — we can write product descriptions, category pages, and homepage copy that is optimised for search and conversion.

Can you add a blog to my e-commerce store?

Yes — and we strongly recommend it. A blog drives free organic traffic through long-tail search queries. Shopify and WooCommerce both have built-in blog functionality that we set up as part of the build.
